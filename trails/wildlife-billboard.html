PLATE · I
White-tailed Deer

White-tailed Deer

Odocoileus virginianus

The flash of a white tail is your signal they've spotted you first. Most active at dawn & dusk. Look for heart-shaped tracks in soft soil.

PLATE · II
Wild Turkey

Wild Turkey

Meleagris gallopavo

Travels the forest floor in small flocks. Listen for the spring gobble; watch for scratched-up leaf litter where they've been feeding.

PLATE · III
Red Fox

Red Fox

Vulpes vulpes

Rust-colored coat, black stockings, white-tipped tail. Curious but cautious. A glimpse is a gift — they rarely linger.

Dusk · NightField Edges
PLATE · IV
Gray Squirrel

Eastern Gray Squirrel

Sciurus carolinensis

The busy cacher of the canopy. Acorns buried and forgotten become next year's oaks — every walk, you're seeing a forest plant itself.

All DayOaks · Hickories
PLATE · V
Raccoon

Raccoon

Procyon lotor

Masked, ringed-tail, and wonderfully clever. Look for hand-like prints near creek banks where they forage for crayfish and frogs.

NocturnalWater · Woods

Cottontail

Eastern Cottontail

Sylvilagus floridanus

A puff of white disappearing into the brush. Freezes before it flees — scan low, still shapes at the edge of the trail.

Pileated Woodpecker

Pileated Woodpecker

Dryocopus pileatus

Crow-sized with a flaming red crest. Listen for a slow, deliberate drumming and a wild, laughing call echoing through the trees.

All DayOld Hardwoods
PLATE · VIII
Barred Owl

Barred Owl

Strix varia

"Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" The deep hollow call is unmistakable — often heard before seen, roosting on mossy branches.

Twilight · NightDeep Woods
PLATE · IX
Box Turtle

Eastern Box Turtle

Terrapene carolina carolina

A slow, living mosaic of orange & black. Please leave them where you find them — they may live a century within a few hundred yards.

After RainDo Not Move

Walk Gently

  • Observe from a distance — if an animal changes its behavior, you are too close.
  • Stay on marked trails to protect nests, burrows, and fragile ground cover.
  • Pack out what you pack in. Leaves alone are a meal to someone.
  • Keep dogs leashed — scent trails echo for hours after you're gone.

The Quiet Hours

The trail is liveliest in the hush of early morning and the gold of late afternoon. Walk slow. Stop often. Most sightings reward the patient wanderer.

Bring binoculars. Leave the phone in your pocket for a while.
