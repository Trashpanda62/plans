A Pocket Almanac of Common Sightings

Eyes on the Trail

Wildlife you may meet along the paths of Tapestry Acres & the woodlands of the Cumberland Plateau
I

The Four-Footed

MAMMALS · 5 SPECIES
White-tailed
Deer

White-tailed Deer

Odocoileus virginianus

The flash of a white tail is your signal they've spotted you first. Most active dawn & dusk.

Red
Fox

Red Fox

Vulpes vulpes

Rust-colored coat, black stockings, white-tipped tail. Curious but cautious — a glimpse is a gift.

Gray
Squirrel

Gray Squirrel

Sciurus carolinensis

The busy cacher of the canopy. Acorns they bury and forget become next year's oaks.

Raccoon

Raccoon

Procyon lotor

Masked and wonderfully clever. Hand-like prints near creek banks where they hunt crayfish.

Eastern
Cottontail

Eastern Cottontail

Sylvilagus floridanus

A puff of white disappearing into the brush. Freezes before it flees — scan low, still shapes.

The Winged

BIRDS · 10 SPECIES
Wild
Turkey

Wild Turkey

Meleagris gallopavo

Travels in small flocks along forest floors. Listen for the spring gobble at dawn.

Pileated
Woodpecker

Pileated Woodpecker

Dryocopus pileatus

Crow-sized with a flaming red crest. A slow, hollow drumming echoes through the trees.

Barred
Owl

Barred Owl

Strix varia

"Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" The hollow call carries through deep woods.

Northern
Cardinal

Northern Cardinal

Cardinalis cardinalis

A scarlet flame in every season. Males sing from high branches to mark their territory.

Blue
Jay

Blue Jay

Cyanocitta cristata

Loud, bold, brilliant blue. Their harsh call warns the whole forest of your approach.

Red-tailed
Hawk

Red-tailed Hawk

Buteo jamaicensis

The raspy scream you hear in movies. Watch for it perched on fenceposts scanning fields.

Eastern
Bluebird

Eastern Bluebird

Sialia sialis

A piece of sky with a rust-red breast. Favors open pastures with scattered perches.

Carolina
Wren

Carolina Wren

Thryothorus ludovicianus

Small, cinnamon, and loud beyond belief. "Teakettle-teakettle-teakettle" all year round.

Red-bellied
Woodpecker

Red-bellied Woodpecker

Melanerpes carolinus

Ladder-backed with a flame-red cap. Misleadingly named — the belly blush is subtle.

Ruby-throated
Hummingbird

Ruby-throated Hummingbird

Archilochus colubris

A living jewel, wings humming at 53 beats per second. Loves jewelweed and bee balm.

The Scaled & Shelled

REPTILES · 10 SPECIES
Eastern Box
Turtle

Eastern Box Turtle

Terrapene carolina

A living mosaic of orange & black. Please leave them — they may live a century within a few hundred yards.

VENOMOUS

Timber Rattlesnake

Crotalus horridus

Chevron bands across a dusky body. Usually warns before striking. Give wide berth, do not harm.

VENOMOUS
Eastern

Eastern Copperhead

Agkistrodon contortrix

Hourglass bands, copper head, often freezes when seen. Watch where you step near logs & leaves.

Black
Rat Snake

Black Rat Snake

Pantherophis alleghaniensis

Long, glossy, non-venomous, and a farmer's ally. Climbs trees to raid nests and hunt rodents.

Eastern
Garter Snake

Eastern Garter Snake

Thamnophis sirtalis

Three yellow stripes on an olive body. Harmless and common in gardens, fields & moist edges.

Northern
Water Snake

Northern Water Snake

Nerodia sipedon

Dark banded, often mistaken for a cottonmouth — but harmless. Sunning on rocks near streams.

Rough
Green Snake

Rough Green Snake

Opheodrys aestivus

Slender, emerald, and gentle. Glides through shrubs like a living vine — almost invisible.

Ring-necked
Snake

Ring-necked Snake

Diadophis punctatus

Pencil-thin with a golden collar and bright-yellow belly. Hides under rocks & old boards.

Five-lined
Skink

Five-lined Skink

Plestiodon fasciatus

Young ones flash a brilliant blue tail. Basks on stone walls and sun-warmed logs.

Eastern
Fence Lizard

Eastern Fence Lizard

Sceloporus undulatus

Bark-colored and spiny, males flash bright-blue bellies. Darts up tree trunks to stay above you.

Walk Gently

  • Observe from a distance — if an animal changes its behavior, you are too close.
  • Stay on marked trails to protect nests, burrows, and fragile cover.
  • Pack out what you pack in. Leave only footprints.
  • Keep dogs leashed — scent trails echo for hours after.
  • Watch for snakes near logs, rocks, and leaf litter, especially on warm mornings.

The Quiet Hours

The trail is liveliest in the hush of early morning and the gold of late afternoon. Walk slow. Stop often. Most sightings reward the patient wanderer.

Bring binoculars. Leave the phone in your pocket.
