The Founding

The Joseph Taylor Grant

The land beneath your feet was part of a sprawling tract purchased by Joseph Taylor in the early 1800s — a holding so large that the crossroads, the road, and the community itself would carry his name for the next two centuries.
❦

Taylor was a man of means and civic standing. After the Cherokee cessions and the organization of Overton County in 1806, settlers like Taylor received large tracts as state land grants — paid for in pennies per acre. Joseph Taylor acquired enough acreage here to constitute its own named district in state records, alongside Monroe, Oakley, Independence, and Ozone.

He deeded a portion of his land for a community school, and the little crossroads took his name in gratitude. His descendants still live on the adjoining parcels today.

Did You Know
Andrew Jackson and John Sevier — Tennessee's first governor — both held land in the Taylor district during this same era.

II · of ten
First Peoples

Hunting Grounds of the Nettle Carrier

Long before Joseph Taylor, before any deed was filed in any courthouse, the hills and hollows you see were a Cherokee hunting preserve — walked by hunters from the Overhill Towns for generations.
❧

The Cherokee who hunted this part of the Upper Cumberland were known in white settlement records as the "Nettle Carrier" Indians, led by a chief of the same name. They maintained friendly relations with early white explorers — including Colonel Stephen Copland, who in 1799 worked out a hospitable arrangement with Nettle Carrier himself to settle near present-day Monroe.

In 1763, a party of Long Hunters — the legendary solo woodsmen who ranged for months at a time — explored these ridges and camped along Spring Creek and the Roaring River. They were very likely the first Europeans to see this land.

The Long Hunters
These early frontiersmen would disappear into the wilderness for up to a year, surviving on game, and returning with pelts worth fortunes back east.

III · of ten
A Forgotten Capital

Monroe, The First County Seat

A few miles from where you stand, Monroe once held the reins of Overton County — a bustling crossroads where the Kentucky Stock Road met the wagon route to Danville.
✦

When Overton County was carved out of Jackson County on September 12, 1806, Monroe was chosen as its seat of government. Benjamin Totten served as the county's first clerk. For nearly thirty years, Monroe thrived as a traffic hub — drovers pushing cattle north to Kentucky, settlers heading west, court days drawing farmers from miles around.

In 1835, the county seat was moved to Livingston. Traffic through Monroe declined; the courthouse left; but the community remained, its bones still visible in the old roads that cross this land.

Neighbors in Name
Joannah Sevier — daughter of Tennessee's first governor — lived most of her life near Monroe and is buried there. Her mother "Bonnie Kate" Sevier moved to Overton County in 1815.

IV · of ten
The War Years

Camp Myers & the Men Who Drilled Here

In the summer of 1861, Confederate recruits from across the Upper Cumberland gathered at two training camps just north of here — one of them, Camp Myers, mere minutes from this property.
⚜

Camp Myers was named for Calvin Myers, a Mexican War veteran who helped recruit several companies for the 25th Tennessee Infantry. He led the regiment until he was mortally wounded in the 1864 Atlanta campaign.

Conditions at the camp were rough. Officers complained of 917 privates with only 665 guns; letters home mentioned no dishes, no soap, beef and flour bread cooked in a shared skillet. And yet from this ground went men who would fight at Perryville, Murfreesboro, Chickamauga — and surrender at Appomattox on April 9, 1865.

A Local Guerrilla
Colonel John M. Hughs of the 25th Tennessee slipped back through these hills in the winter of 1863–64, leading a 300-man Confederate guerrilla band into Kentucky to raid Federal installations.

V · of ten
The Heart of the Crossroads

Fourteen Pairs of Mules

In 1919, the community did something remarkable. They picked up an entire general store — building and all — and moved it across miles of swamp and field to the crossroads.
❦

The original store had stood in the Hatcher Hall community, owned first by Joe D. Hatcher and later by Hardy Keisling. In 1919, the entire building was mounted on poplar poles and dragged by fourteen pairs of mules to its new home at Taylors Crossroads — passing through swampland owned by the Harris Hatcher family and across lands of Paul and William Sells.

For the next ninety-one years, this store was the beating heart of the community — until its doors closed for the last time on September 2, 2010.

The Coleman Era
John Dudley Coleman bought the store in 1930. The Coleman family — Bill, Ed, Minor, J.D., and their sons — would own and operate it for the next sixty-one years.

VI · of ten
A Farming Community

The Six Corn Mills

Six corn mills once spun within walking distance of this spot — each one run by a neighbor, each one grinding the sustenance of a community that fed itself.
❧

The millers were names still familiar here: Sam Flowers. Roscoe Martin. Sherman Garrett. Billy Morgan Garrett. Virgil Stover. Bob Taylor. Each ran a small mill grinding corn into meal for cornbread and hominy, and into cracked feed for hogs and chickens.

Farmers on this land raised corn as the staple crop, with tobacco — both burley and dark-fired — as the cash crop that paid the taxes. Cattle grazed the hollows; hogs rooted in the woods; chickens scratched the dooryards. It was a landscape that asked much of its people and gave back enough, if just enough, to keep families here for two hundred years.

Honoring the Past
These 110 acres continue the tradition — now with alpacas, goats, a mini donkey, and a mini horse instead of draft mules and plow oxen. Same land, different stewards.

VII · of ten
Lean Years

Six Cents a Dozen

During the Great Depression, cash disappeared from these hills almost entirely. Farmers bartered with what they had — eggs, chickens, chestnuts, ginseng, fur hides — and took "due bills" instead of dollars.
✦

Bill Coleman remembered the prices. Eggs were six cents a dozen. Corn, forty cents a bushel. One legendary trade at the general store: a Steel King double-bladed axe, priced at $1.60, swapped even-up for a 500-pound calf.

Children dug May Apple root to dry and sell. They scoured the woods for ginseng — worth far more than any other foraged good. They picked up glass Coke bottles along the roads and cashed them in at the store. Everyone in the community, down to the youngest child, contributed something to the family ledger.

A Resourceful Place
Country hams aged on wires strung from poles in the side room of the store. To test one, the storekeeper would stick a nail into the ham and smell it to see if it was still good.

VIII · of ten
Those Who Came Before

The Family Cemetery

Please walk softly here. The small burying ground nearby is one of the eight old family cemeteries clustered around Taylors Crossroads — the quiet record of a community that lived, worked, and rested on this land.
✝

The nearby Taylors Crossroads area contains the resting places of the founding families: Sells, Stover, Miller, Richardson, Conner, Taylor, Smith, and Reeder. Some markers date from the mid-1800s. Some are weathered beyond reading. Others — fieldstones with no inscription at all — mark infants and early settlers whose names never made it into any ledger.

Together, the graveyards around Taylors Crossroads hold more than two hundred souls. They were farmers, millers, storekeepers, mothers who outlived too many children, Confederate veterans, and Sunday-school teachers. They made this place what it is.

Please Respect the Ground
Take only photographs. Leave flowers, not footprints on the graves. Many of these families still have descendants living within a mile of this spot.

IX · of ten
The Old Meetinghouse

A Church in the Hollow

At the far edge of the property stands a small, weathered church — silent now, its paint long gone, its congregation scattered to newer buildings or laid down beside it in the earth.
✧

Rural Overton County was once dotted with these small meetinghouses — one-room sanctuaries built by hand, using local timber, on land deeded by whichever neighbor could spare an acre. The congregations were Baptist, Methodist, Cumberland Presbyterian, and — most numerous here — Church of Christ.

Sunday mornings meant families walking in from every direction along the crossroads, children in their best clothes, men shaking hands in the churchyard, the preacher's voice carrying through open windows in warm weather. When the roads improved and the congregations merged into larger churches in town, buildings like this one simply fell quiet.

Still Standing
The building itself is a piece of folk architecture — timber joinery, hand-planed siding, and a roof that has outlasted every generation of the community it served.

X · of ten
The Land Today

One Hundred and Three Acres

What you see is the newest chapter in a very long story — a story that stretches back through general stores and corn mills, through Civil War camps and courthouses, through Cherokee hunting grounds and into the deep quiet of the pre-colonial forest.
❦

Today, Tapestry Acres raises heritage-breed alpacas, goats, a mini horse named Penelope, and a mini donkey named Murphy. The fiber is spun into yarn. The pastures are managed for both grazing and hay. The property welcomes visitors for bookable experiences — because the best way to honor a piece of land with this much history is to let it keep working, keep teaching, and keep gathering people together.

Thank you for walking with us. The Taylor family named the crossroads; the Coleman family kept the store; the Sells, Stovers, and Garretts ground the corn. We are only the latest stewards. The land remembers them all.
